# Community And Social Research

Use this reference before researching Facebook groups, forums, Reddit, TikTok comments, Instagram comments, YouTube comments, product reviews, or other community conversations for GRUBZ marketing ideas.

## Research Boundaries

- Use public sources or user-provided excerpts/screenshots.
- Do not scrape private Facebook groups or bypass platform access controls.
- Do not reveal names, profiles, private group names, locations, photos, or identifying details from ordinary users.
- Do not copy posts verbatim into marketing assets.
- Summarize patterns, questions, objections, hooks, and content structures.
- If source access requires a logged-in session, ask the user to provide screenshots, exports, links, or permission to use an available browser session.

## Facebook Group Research Workflow

1. Define the audience: backyard chicken keepers, homesteaders, small flock owners, poultry hobbyists, or pet chicken owners.
2. If the user provides many approved Facebook groups, load them from a Google Sheet, `inputs/facebook-groups.csv`, or a pasted list of group URLs/names.
3. Prioritize groups by relevance, language, region, activity, and fit for GRUBZ. Sample across the list instead of overfitting to one large group.
4. Collect a small sample of high-engagement public posts, visible posts from a user-approved browser session, or user-provided group examples.
5. For 20+ groups, aim for breadth first: 2-5 useful high-engagement observations per group, then deepen into the strongest recurring themes.
6. For each post, record only non-identifying observations:
   - Group row/id
   - Topic
   - Hook style
   - Visual type
   - Engagement prompt
   - Emotion
   - Problem or aspiration
   - Comments pattern
   - Product relevance
7. Cluster findings into themes.
8. Convert themes into original GRUBZ social ideas.
9. Add a claim-safety pass before final copy.

## 40-Group Research Plan

Use this plan when the user provides a large group list:

1. Intake: confirm each group has a name or URL, audience, language, country/region, and notes where available.
2. Access: verify whether the user will provide screenshots/exports or permission to use a logged-in browser session.
3. Sampling: review the newest posts and the most visibly engaged posts available in each group.
4. Coding: tag observations by theme, hook, visual, engagement prompt, objection, and GRUBZ relevance.
5. Synthesis: produce a ranked insight list with evidence counts across groups.
6. Ideation: create original GRUBZ posts from the strongest patterns.
7. Calendar: optionally turn the ideas into a weekly/monthly publishing plan.

Never present private group observations as public citations. Use source labels like "Group 07, chicken enrichment thread" when the source is private or user-approved.

## Group List Input

Preferred source: a Google Sheet shared by the user.

Fallback file: `inputs/facebook-groups.csv`.

Columns:

- `id`: short stable id, such as `G01`.
- `group_name`: group name.
- `url`: Facebook group URL.
- `language`: primary language.
- `country_region`: country or region focus.
- `audience`: backyard chickens, homesteading, poultry health, egg layers, etc.
- `access`: approved, public, screenshot-only, export-only, unknown.
- `priority`: high, medium, low.
- `notes`: anything useful, such as "very active", "Greek keepers", "strict no sales".

Google Sheet setup:

1. Create one tab named `facebook_groups`.
2. Use the column names above in row 1.
3. Put one Facebook group per row.
4. Share the sheet link with the agent, or export/download it as CSV if direct Google Sheets access is unavailable.
5. If using a connector or browser session, verify the sheet is accessible before starting research.

If the user provides a Google Sheet link but no Google Sheets/Drive connector is available, ask the user to either install/connect Google Drive or export the `facebook_groups` tab as CSV and place it at `inputs/facebook-groups.csv`.

If the user pastes raw links instead of filling a sheet or file, normalize them into this structure before research.

## What To Look For In Successful Chicken Posts

- Practical questions: feeding, treats, eggshells, molting, boredom, pests, weather, coop routines, flock hierarchy.
- Relatable moments: funny flock behavior, feeding-time excitement, picky hens, daily chores, first-time keeper mistakes.
- Visual proof: before/after setups, flock reactions, close-up feed shots, egg baskets, coop scenes.
- Participation prompts: "What would you do?", "Show your flock", "Name this behavior", "Guess what happened next."
- Education hooks: myth vs fact, quick tips, mistakes to avoid, "did you know" posts.
- Seasonal hooks: heat, cold, molting season, spring chicks, holiday treats, rainy-day enrichment.
- Trust cues: personal experience, simple routines, low-mess tips, natural behavior.

## Translate Research Into GRUBZ Ideas

For each idea, provide:

- Source insight: a short theme summary, not a copied post.
- Audience: who it is for.
- Hook: first line or first 2 seconds.
- Format: reel, carousel, photo post, story poll, short, or live demo.
- Visual direction: what to film or photograph.
- Caption: original GRUBZ copy.
- CTA: comment, save, share, shop, or ask a question.
- Engagement mechanic: poll, question, challenge, duet/stitch, comment prompt, or UGC request.
- Claim notes: any claims to avoid or substantiate.

## Strong Chicken-Specific GRUBZ Angles

- "Treat time as enrichment, not just snacks."
- "A natural insect snack for backyard flocks."
- "Foraging behavior in a handful."
- "Made in Greece for keepers who want a simple, trusted treat."
- "Easy to store, easy to sprinkle, exciting for the flock."
- "A protein-rich treat alongside a balanced chicken feed."
